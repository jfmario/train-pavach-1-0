Characters

Regular Characters

Characters are formed by combining a consonant and a vowel. For example, the consonant mān (m) and the vowel sā (ā) combine to form the character mā.

+ =

As another example, the consonant sāz (s) and the vowel sū (ū) combine to form the syllable sū, which happens to be the name of that vowel.

+ =

A vowel and a consonant combine to make a character. A character is any consonant-vowel combination, even if the consonant or vowel is blank. If the consonant place is not blank and the vowel is not the silent yeŋ, the character is called a regular character.

Lone Consonants

Characters consisting of only a consonant accompaning by a yeŋ are called lone consonants or consonantal characters. They are not permitted at the beginning of words. They only appear at the end of words or prior to a regular character (a character comprised of a normal consonant and a normal vowel). There are no circumstances in which two lone consonants can appear consecutively.

Consider the two syllables of my first name, rāk-san.

(rāk)

(san)

(rāksan)

The first syllable of my name is composed of a regular character (made of the consonant r and the vowel ā) and the lone consonant k. The second syllable is composed of the regular character sa and the lone consonant n.

Lone Vowels

Vowels can appear by themselves (without a preceding consonant) only at the beginning of words. This is because a vowel following a consonant is attached to it and two vowels cannot appear in a row. Consider the name odəram.

Lone Consonants and Lone Vowels in Foreign Words

The only exceptions to the rules of lone consonants and lone vowels happen when foreign words are transcribed. Consider the foreign names Mark and Lois. If a Pavach writer were addressing a letter, the names would probably be written like this:

(mārk)

(lois)

However, if those names became popular and were imported in to our culture, they would take the following forms to comply with our norms:

(mārək, with the insignificant short vowel fərāʕ added between r and k)

(loʕis, with the glottal stop ʕeʕ added between o and i)

Lois would not be imported as a female name anyways. It would either be a male name or changed to losi or loʕi. For more on why, see the culture section later in this chapter.

Culture: Names

Male and Female First Names

The main rule for first names is that they must be more than one syllable long and they are almost always less than four syllables long.

Male first names must end in a closed syllable (cvc - consonant, vowel, consonant) and female names must end in an open syllable (cv). This rule is sometimes ignored in the north.

Common forms for male names are: cvccvc, vcvcvc, and vcvccvc. Common forms for female names are vcv, cvcv, and vcvcv.

The last syllable of a name is a familiar nickname. Close friends of mine will call me San. When I was in the Castle Guard, my equal would call me San, but my superiors would call me Raksan.

Last Names

Last names are formed out of the familiar names of the two parents. Most of the time this is done by having the father's familiar name added to the end of the mother's familiar name. For example, a mother named Fara and a father named Shelwag would yield the last name Rawag.

This means that the usual form of a last name is 'cvcvc'. However, it is possible to add the mother's name to the end of the father's name, in the form 'cvccv'.

My last name, Mayli (mælī) actually follows the latter form. My father's name was onikmæl and my mother's name was šālī. Pavach writing ignores two consonants in a row if there is no vowel between them. Unlike many other languages, there is no distinction between sounds like yellow and yelow. If a combination of two words or the addition of a prefix or suffix woould result in two consonants in a row like that, we write the consonant only once. This is why there is only one l in my name.
